Analysis of HPT Cases

Case Analysis 1: Distance Education Faculty Performance Improvement

Background

Universities in Turkey were experiencing significant performance deficiencies among distance education (DE) faculty members. Despite the growing importance of online learning, faculty struggled with various barriers that impacted their effectiveness in delivering quality distance education. The challenges were multifaceted, affecting not only faculty performance but also student outcomes and institutional effectiveness. A key faculty statement that summarized their experience was "I learn by trial and error," highlighting the lack of systematic support and guidance.

HPT Analysis Process

Kara and Yildirim employed David Wile's Externality-Tangibility (E-T) model to conduct a comprehensive front-end analysis. This model examines performance deficiencies through external and internal factors, with external factors further categorized into tangible and intangible elements. The researchers collected qualitative data through interviews, observations, and document reviews from DE experts, faculty, administrators, students, and support staff.

The analysis revealed nine interconnected categories of performance deficiency: Talents (including low motivation and lack of commitment to DE), Knowledge/Skills (insufficient andragogy knowledge and communication skills), Tools (need for appropriate LMS and insufficient bandwidth), Environment (shared offices and lesson disruptions), Incentives (unsatisfactory payment structures), Information (hesitation to communicate and lack of student characteristic data), Job Aids (unused LMS guides and ineffective student orientation), Management (self-directed working and insufficient feedback), and Leadership (insufficient teamwork and change challenges).

Key Findings and Systemic Issues

The study revealed that performance deficiencies were deeply rooted in national and institutional rules and regulations. The interconnected nature of the identified barriers meant that addressing individual factors in isolation would not create significant performance improvement. Instead, the analysis showed that all factors needed to be addressed concurrently at both institutional and national levels to create meaningful change.

Intervention Implications

While this study focused on identifying causes (Part I), the systematic analysis using Wile's E-T model provided a foundation for comprehensive intervention design. The researchers concluded that institutions must be held accountable to their vision and supported by all stakeholders. The study emphasized that without addressing systemic issues, faculty would continue to focus on individual development rather than collective organizational improvement.

Case Analysis 2: Academic Needs Assessment for Faculty Performance Data

Background

Swart and Kaufman addressed a critical challenge in academic performance improvement: how to convert subjective qualitative data about faculty perceptions into actionable, measurable quantitative data for decision-making. Universities often struggle to identify and address faculty performance gaps in ways that lead to concrete, measurable improvements rather than simply providing resources based on wants rather than needs.

HPT Analysis Process

The researchers developed an Academic Needs Assessment (ANA) survey specifically designed to identify performance gaps that faculty perceived about their leadership, environment, and colleagues. This methodology differed from traditional "wants assessments" by focusing on objectives that departments could accomplish and evaluate within a specific timeframe, rather than simply identifying desired means and resources.

The approach demonstrated results-oriented thinking by carefully designing a process that could quantify faculty perceptions and convert them into measurable data points. The researchers were able to assign value to qualitative statements, creating a systematic framework for understanding performance discrepancies between current state and desired outcomes.

Methodology and Framework

The ANA survey methodology was specifically tailored to the unique academic environment, focusing on developing performance data that could inform useful faculty and leadership decisions. The process involved collecting perception data from faculty about various performance factors and then working with department heads to discuss findings and identify actionable improvement opportunities.

Analysis and Next Steps

While the study successfully demonstrated how to quantify faculty perceptions and identify performance gaps, it primarily focused on problem identification rather than solution implementation. The logical next step in this performance improvement process would involve applying Gilbert's Behavior Engineering Model (BEM) to identify the specific causes of performance discrepancies and pinpoint environmental and behavioral factors related to information, resources, and motivation that could guide intervention design.

The study's strength lies in its systematic approach to converting subjective perceptions into objective, measurable data that can inform evidence-based decision-making in academic environments. This methodology provides department heads with concrete data they need to successfully implement targeted performance improvement solutions.

Personal Reflection on HPT Case Analysis

Through conducting these HPT case analyses, I have gained a deeper appreciation for the complexity of performance improvement and the value of systematic analysis. The most significant learning has been understanding how often performance problems have root causes that are not immediately obvious and how environmental factors frequently have greater impact than individual factors.

The case analyses have reinforced the importance of resisting the temptation to jump to solutions before thoroughly understanding the problem. This systematic approach not only leads to more effective interventions but also helps organizations avoid costly mistakes and wasted resources.

In my future practice, I plan to utilize HPT case analysis as a cornerstone of my performance improvement approach. The frameworks and methodologies provide a robust foundation for understanding performance problems and designing effective interventions. I also recognize the importance of developing strong analytical skills and the ability to see performance issues from multiple perspectives.

The experience has also highlighted the importance of stakeholder engagement throughout the analysis process. Performance improvement is not just a technical exercise but requires buy-in and collaboration from multiple levels of the organization. This understanding will guide my approach to future HPT projects and client relationships.
